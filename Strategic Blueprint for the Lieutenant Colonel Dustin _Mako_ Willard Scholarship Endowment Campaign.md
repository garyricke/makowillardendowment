# **Strategic Blueprint for the Lieutenant Colonel Dustin "Mako" Willard Scholarship Endowment Campaign**

## **Executive Summary and Campaign Thesis**

The transition of a highly decorated military aviator from active operational service to retirement presents a unique, emotionally resonant catalyst for philanthropic mobilization. Lieutenant Colonel Dustin "Mako" Willard is culminating a distinguished, two-decade career in the United States Air Force, a tenure characterized by exceptional leadership, extensive combat experience, and command of the nation’s most sensitive high-altitude reconnaissance operations.1 As his military career draws to a close with his ceremonial "Fini Flight" scheduled for April 9th, an unprecedented opportunity exists to translate his legacy of service into a permanent educational endowment at his alma mater, Cedar Rapids Kennedy High School.1

This comprehensive strategic report outlines the exact architectural blueprint required to launch a successful capitalization campaign for the newly established endowment. Operating under the fiduciary umbrella of the Cedar Rapids Community Schools Foundation (CRCSF), this initiative will intertwine the prestige of military service with the localized pride of the Cedar Rapids Kennedy High School community.2 By leveraging the momentum of the Fini Flight, the campaign will capture the attention of military colleagues, aerospace industry partners, local alumni, and philanthropic organizations.4 The ensuing sections provide an exhaustive analysis of Lieutenant Colonel Willard’s biography, the institutional mechanics of the endowment, a cohesive brand identity guide, digital infrastructure recommendations, comprehensive website copy, and a phased social media mobilization strategy designed to secure the required capital to fund the scholarship in perpetuity.

## **Biographical Foundation: A Career of Altitude and Impact**

A philanthropic campaign rooted in the legacy of an individual must accurately and compellingly articulate the scope of their achievements to potential donors. Lieutenant Colonel Willard’s trajectory from an Iowa student to a senior commander of global reconnaissance assets provides a powerful narrative framework for donor engagement and establishes the core criteria for the scholarship's future recipients.

### **Academic Origins and Commissioning**

Dustin R. Willard’s foundational years were rooted in Cedar Rapids, Iowa, where he developed the academic discipline and leadership traits that would define his military career.1 Following his graduation from Cedar Rapids Kennedy High School, a public institution founded in 1967 and known for its "Cougars" mascot and green and gold heraldry, he pursued higher education at the University of Iowa.1 He earned a Bachelor of Science in Chemical Engineering in 2006, demonstrating an early aptitude for highly technical and analytical problem-solving.1 Crucially, he earned his commission through the university's Reserve Officer Training Corps (ROTC), distinguishing himself as a Distinguished Graduate of Detachment 255\.1

This academic rigor did not conclude with his commissioning; it was a continuous thread throughout his service. He completed the Air and Space Basic Course in 2006, the Squadron Officer School in 2013, and the Aviation Mishap Investigation Course at Dyess Air Force Base in 2016\.1 His strategic education was further supplemented by a Master’s degree in Military Operational Art and Sciences from the Air Command and Staff College at Maxwell Air Force Base in 2017, and subsequent advanced education at the Army Command and General Staff College at Fort Leavenworth in 2018\.1 This documented trajectory from a local Cedar Rapids student to a strategic military thinker forms the central ethos of the proposed scholarship: empowering the next generation of highly capable, technically proficient leaders from the local community.

### **Tactical and Strategic Aviation Progression**

Following his commissioning as a Second Lieutenant in May 2006, Lieutenant Colonel Willard embarked on a rigorous aviation career that would span multiple airframes and theaters of operation.1 His initial assignment as an Executive Officer for the 349th Air Refueling Squadron at McConnell Air Force Base laid the groundwork for his operational understanding before he entered Undergraduate Pilot Training at the 47th Flying Training Wing at Laughlin Air Force Base, Texas, in 2007\.1

His aviation career rapidly ascended as he was selected to pilot the B-1B Lancer, a supersonic variable-sweep wing heavy bomber central to the United States' strategic deterrence and conventional strike capabilities.1 Following Initial Qualification Training at the 28th Bomb Squadron, he was stationed with the 9th Bomb Squadron at Dyess Air Force Base from 2009 to 2013\.1 During this tenure, he served not merely as a pilot, but advanced through the rigorous qualifications of Aircraft Commander, Mission Lead, Assistant Flight Commander, Flight Lead, and Instructor Pilot.1 This multi-faceted role in one of the Air Force's most lethal and complex strategic assets underscores his capacity for high-stakes decision-making and mentorship of junior aviators.

In April 2013, Willard demonstrated profound operational versatility by transitioning into the intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) domain, taking command as an MC-12W Mission Commander.1 Serving with the 427th Reconnaissance Squadron at Beale Air Force Base, California, he also functioned as the Assistant Chief of Weapons and Tactics and Flight Commander.1 This pivot from strategic bombing to tactical ISR required a fundamental shift in operational mindset, directly supporting ground forces through real-time intelligence gathering in hostile and dynamic environments.

### **The Dragon Lady Era and Senior Command**

The pinnacle of Lieutenant Colonel Willard's flying career is undeniably defined by his association with the U-2S Dragon Lady. The U-2 is an aircraft notorious for being one of the most physically and technically difficult to fly in the world, operating at the edge of space at altitudes exceeding 70,000 feet, necessitating a specialized full-pressure suit for the pilot.6 Entering the U-2S program as a student pilot in 2015 at the 1st Reconnaissance Squadron at Beale Air Force Base, he quickly mastered the airframe.1 He moved to the 99th Reconnaissance Squadron where he served as a U-2S Pilot and Safety Officer, and eventually advanced to the role of U-2S Instructor Pilot and Evaluator Pilot, alongside serving as the Weapons Instructor Course Development Branch Chief for Wing Tactics within the 9th Operational Support Squadron.1

His leadership capabilities evolved in tandem with his elite flying expertise. He transitioned into high-level strategic planning roles, serving as the Deputy Chief of Reconnaissance Operations within the Combat Operations Division of the 613th Air Operations Center, and later as the Deputy Branch Chief of Reconnaissance Operations at Pacific Air Force Headquarters at Joint Base Pearl Harbor-Hickam, Hawaii.1 In these roles, he navigated the highly complex geopolitical theaters of the INDOPACOM area of responsibility.1

Returning to the operational hub of Beale Air Force Base, he assumed command of the 9th Operations Support Squadron from May 2022 to June 2024\.1 In this capacity, he led a diverse and highly technical team of 150 officer, enlisted, and civilian personnel across nine distinct career fields to drive global reconnaissance operations on a 24/7 basis.1 His squadron was responsible for the entirety of flight operations at Beale, producing wing intelligence, managing flight records, ensuring communications security, and providing critical base weather support.1

Currently, Lieutenant Colonel Willard serves as the Deputy Commander of the 9th Operations Group at Beale Air Force Base, an immense responsibility he assumed in June 2024\.1 In this executive leadership capacity, he directs over 1,000 personnel distributed across 11 units.1 He oversees daily operations for an $8 billion fleet comprising U-2 and T-38 aircraft, managing a $275 million flight hour program.1 His operational mandate includes executing 3,000 U-2 sorties annually, totaling 13,500 flight hours in direct, critical support of the President of the United States (POTUS), the Secretary of Defense (SECDEF), and various combatant commanders.1 Furthermore, he is the executive responsible for the Common Mission Control Center program, which commands an annual budget exceeding $1.6 billion.1

## **The Symbology of the Fini Flight and U-2 Divestment**

To construct a deeply resonant fundraising campaign, the external messaging must lean heavily into the symbolic, visual, and emotional milestones of Lieutenant Colonel Willard's retirement. The primary event anchoring this campaign is his scheduled Fini Flight on April 9th, an event that carries amplified historical weight due to the impending retirement of the U-2 platform itself.

### **The Imminent Retirement of the Dragon Lady**

The U-2 Dragon Lady, a vital intelligence-gathering asset since the Cold War, is currently undergoing a phased divestment by the United States Air Force, with complete retirement scheduled for 2026\.6 Beale Air Force Base has already begun retiring specific airframes, such as tail numbers 1065 and 80-1085, after decades of service and tens of thousands of flight hours.6 Furthermore, Beale recently processed its final wet film optical bar camera (OBC) mission, transitioning the U-2 entirely into the digital age just prior to its overall divestment.7 Lieutenant Colonel Willard’s retirement is therefore not just the conclusion of an individual career, but it coincides with the twilight of one of the most legendary aviation programs in military history. This dual sunset provides a profound narrative hook for the endowment campaign, framing the scholarship as a way to preserve a piece of aerospace history that is rapidly fading.

### **The Significance of the April 9th Fini Flight**

The "Fini Flight" is a deeply revered, highly emotional tradition within military aviation, tracing its historical roots back to the Army Air Forces in World War II.8 It marks an aviator's final departure from a squadron, a specific aircraft, or active service altogether. At Beale Air Force Base, the Fini Flight of a Dragon Lady pilot is a profound ceremonial event, recognizing the elite nature of the U-2 community.6

As the pilot descends from the edge of space, navigating the U-2's notoriously challenging bicycle-gear landing sequence one last time, they are met on the flight line by a gathering of family, squadron mates, base leadership, and chase-car drivers.6 Upon exiting the aircraft, the retiring aviator is traditionally hosed down with water from a fire truck or by squadron members wielding hoses and champagne bottles.4

Scheduling the launch of the endowment campaign concurrently with the April 9th Fini Flight creates a powerful psychological trigger for potential donors. It captures a highly targeted audience—military peers, defense contractors, alumni, and community members—at a moment of peak emotional engagement. The visual assets generated on this day, including the U-2 taxiing with its pogo wheels, the water salute, and the final handshake on the tarmac, will serve as the premier marketing collateral for the campaign, symbolizing the transition of his legacy from the stratosphere back to the academic grounds of Cedar Rapids.

## **The "Mako" Callsign: Symbolism and Brand Anchor**

Lieutenant Colonel Willard’s tactical callsign, "Mako," provides an exceptional branding anchor for the endowment campaign. In the culture of military aviation, callsigns are earned identifiers that reflect a pilot's character, operational style, or specific notable incidents.10

The Mako shark is nature's fastest shark, a high-speed apex predator known for its extraordinary intelligence, precision, agility, and streamlined lethality.11 Within military aviation nomenclature, the term "Mako" has historically been utilized to denote precision, advanced adversary tactics, and elite operations.10 For example, Navy adversary squadrons have utilized "Mako" camouflage schemes to simulate elite peer-adversary fighters, and the callsign has been historically associated with high-stakes special operations.12

By actively leveraging the "Mako" moniker in the campaign branding, the endowment shifts from a standard, passive academic fund into a dynamic, character-driven initiative. The name embodies speed, precision, intelligence, and an unwavering commitment to the mission—the exact core values that the scholarship will seek to identify, cultivate, and reward in future Cedar Rapids Kennedy High School graduates.

## **Institutional Framework: Endowment Mechanics and the CRCSF**

To ensure the perpetual impact of the Lieutenant Colonel Dustin "Mako" Willard Scholarship, the financial and legal structure must be meticulously aligned with the Cedar Rapids Community Schools Foundation (CRCSF). The campaign must articulate these mechanics transparently and professionally to assure major donors of the fund’s long-term sustainability, legal compliance, and fiduciary oversight.

### **Fiduciary Governance within the CRCSF**

The CRCSF is a registered 501(c)(3) tax-exempt organization designed specifically to build financial support, promote excellence, and enhance equitable educational experiences throughout the Cedar Rapids Public Schools system.3 It operates entirely independently of the public district budget, which is strictly allocated for operational expenses such as salaries and building maintenance.3 Establishing the scholarship through the Foundation shields the capital from public budgetary fluctuations and ensures strict compliance with applicable legal frameworks, including the Iowa Uniform Prudent Management of Institutional Funds Act (UPMIFA).15

By partnering with the Foundation, the campaign organizers relieve the Willard family of all administrative burdens, complex tax reporting, and ongoing investment management.16 The Foundation assumes full responsibility for critical backend operations, including financial reporting, official gift acknowledgment for IRS deduction purposes, vendor payments, and ensuring that no-fee electronic donation processing is available for campaign contributors.16

Furthermore, the CRCSF maintains a highly structured, risk-averse investment policy. The Foundation categorizes assets into three distinct pools:

1. **Short-Term Funds:** Highly liquid assets designed to cover at least six months of operational and grant expenses, protected by FDIC insurance.15  
2. **Intermediate-Term Funds:** Investments selected to provide liquidity for expenses up to five years, often used for restricted donations.15  
3. **Long-Term (Endowment) Funds:** Permanently endowed assets from which distributions of the principal are strictly prohibited. These are invested in a diversified portfolio to produce long-term growth and generate current income for scholarship programs.15

Donors must be made aware that the Foundation retains a standard 5% administrative fee on all managed funds to cover its operational overhead, a vital metric that must be factored into the campaign's overall capitalization goal.16 Additionally, the Foundation operates under strict conflict-of-interest policies, requiring full disclosure and recusal from board members regarding any transactions that could result in personal benefit, ensuring the highest standards of ethical stewardship of donor capital.15

### **Financial Thresholds and Capitalization Strategy**

To transition a scholarship from a temporary, expendable annual award to a permanent, named endowment, specific financial minimums must be met. While exact requirements can vary depending on the specific agreement negotiated with the Foundation, comparable regional community foundations—such as the Greater Cedar Rapids Community Foundation (GCRCF) and nearby academic institutions in Iowa—uniformly require a minimum corpus threshold of $25,000 to establish a permanently endowed fund.17

Therefore, the primary objective of the April 9th launch campaign is to cross the $25,000 threshold to secure endowed status.17

### **Scholarship Award Criteria and Disbursement Logistics**

The parameters of the scholarship will be established collaboratively between the donors and the CRCSF.16 The Foundation currently manages over 80 senior scholarships, such as the Colin Taylor Memorial Scholarship and the Andrew Seiler Memorial Scholarship at Kennedy High School, providing an established framework for administration.16

Given Lieutenant Colonel Willard’s background, the following criteria matrix is recommended to ensure the recipient reflects his legacy:

1. **Academic Excellence:** A stringent minimum GPA requirement (e.g., 3.5), honoring Willard’s distinction as an engineering graduate and ROTC Distinguished Graduate.1  
2. **Field of Study Alignment:** Preference given to graduating Kennedy High School seniors pursuing degrees in STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics), aviation, or those entering a collegiate ROTC program or federal military academy.  
3. **Leadership and Character:** Demonstrated leadership within school activities, athletic teams, or community service, echoing the spirit of the Kennedy Cougars.2

The selection process will be administered locally by the high school counseling department in conjunction with the Foundation to ensure unbiased, compliant selection.16 The inaugural award should ideally be presented at the Kennedy High School Senior Awards Night, bringing the campaign full circle.23
